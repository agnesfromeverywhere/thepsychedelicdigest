Psychedelic therapy — the basics
Psychedelic-assisted therapy is a clinical treatment model in which a psychedelic substance such as psilocybin, MDMA, or ketamine is administered in a controlled therapeutic setting under the supervision of trained clinicians. The experience is used as a catalyst for psychological processing, typically combined with preparatory and integration sessions. It differs from recreational use in its structured, intention-led, and clinically supervised context.
A standard psychedelic therapy session involves three phases: preparation, the dosing session, and integration. Preparation typically involves one or more sessions with the therapist to establish trust, set intentions, and screen for contraindications. The dosing session itself usually lasts 4 to 8 hours in a calm clinical environment, with the therapist present throughout. Integration involves follow-up sessions to process and apply insights from the experience. Protocols vary by substance and clinic.
Current clinical research is most advanced for treatment-resistant depression (psilocybin), PTSD (MDMA), end-of-life anxiety (psilocybin), alcohol use disorder, and obsessive-compulsive disorder. Research is also expanding into chronic pain, eating disorders, and other conditions. Leading centres include Johns Hopkins, NYU, Imperial College London, and Monash University.
Duration of effects varies by substance, condition, and individual. Psilocybin trials for depression have shown sustained remission in over 50% of participants at six months, with some studies reporting positive outcomes up to 12 months after a single dose. MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD has shown lasting symptom relief in 71% of participants at 12-month follow-up. These are clinical trial results and may not reflect outcomes in all settings. Ongoing integration work is associated with longer-lasting benefits.
This is one of the most contested and underresearched questions in the field. Despite the widespread assumption that psychotherapeutic support is essential, there has been surprisingly little empirical study of the therapy component in isolation. Some research suggests that subjective psychedelic effects may not be necessary for therapeutic outcomes and that non-drug factors may be doing significant work. Separating pharmacological from psychological mechanisms remains a major methodological challenge. The field does not yet have a definitive answer.
Access and legal status
Yes, with restrictions. As of July 2023, the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) rescheduled MDMA and psilocybin for use by authorised prescribers, making Australia the first country in the world to formally regulate these substances for therapeutic use. Treatment is only available through authorised psychiatrists and approved clinics. Recreational possession and use remain illegal.
Australia rescheduled psilocybin and MDMA for therapeutic use in July 2023 through a decision by the Therapeutic Goods Administration, making it the first country to formally regulate both substances for clinical use. The US FDA rejected the MDMA application in 2024, citing concerns about study design. Australia's regulatory pathway was facilitated by a more centralised regulatory system, strong clinical advocacy, and the TGA's willingness to act on existing trial evidence before Phase 3 US completion.
Legal access varies significantly by country and region. In Australia, authorised psychiatrists can prescribe psilocybin and MDMA as of July 2023. In the United States, Oregon and Colorado have regulated psilocybin service programmes. For clinical trials, ClinicalTrials.gov lists active studies. Reputable resources include the Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS) and the Zendo Project. Always verify a practitioner's credentials and regulatory standing before engaging with any service.
Costs vary widely. Ketamine therapy in the US typically costs $400 to $800 per session. Psilocybin therapy in Oregon currently costs $1,000 to $3,000 per session. Insurance coverage is extremely limited and largely unavailable outside of clinical trial settings as of 2025. Cost and access remain among the most significant barriers to widespread adoption of psychedelic-assisted therapy.
Access and cost are among the most significant ethical challenges facing the field. Time-intensive sessions requiring trained professionals represent a major economic barrier to implementing psychedelic therapy in public health systems. Without insurance coverage or public funding pathways, psychedelic therapy risks becoming available primarily to affluent populations, which contradicts the mental health equity goals many advocates associate with the field.
This is an active policy debate. Proponents argue that the cost-effectiveness of psychedelic therapy, particularly given its potential for durable outcomes after few sessions, makes public coverage economically justifiable. Critics point to the current lack of Phase 3 regulatory approval in most jurisdictions, the resource intensity of the therapy model, and unresolved questions about scalability. No major public health system currently covers psychedelic-assisted therapy as standard care.
Microdosing
Microdosing is the practice of consuming a sub-perceptual dose of a psychedelic substance, typically psilocybin mushrooms or LSD, on a structured schedule. A sub-perceptual dose is small enough that it does not produce hallucinations or significantly impair normal functioning, but may produce subtle shifts in mood, focus, creativity, or emotional regulation. Common schedules include the Fadiman protocol (one day on, two days off) and the Stamets protocol.
The scientific literature on microdosing is still emerging. Some observational and self-report studies suggest potential benefits for mood, focus, and anxiety. However, rigorous double-blind placebo-controlled trials are limited, and early results show significant placebo effects. Researchers at Imperial College London and the University of Toronto have published preliminary findings. The evidence is promising but not yet conclusive. Microdosing should not be treated as a substitute for professional mental health care.
A full psychedelic dose produces significant alterations in perception, cognition, and emotion, typically lasting 4 to 8 hours. A microdose is approximately 5 to 10 percent of a full dose and is designed to be sub-perceptual, meaning the user can function normally throughout the day. Full doses are associated with therapeutic breakthrough experiences in clinical settings, while microdosing is associated with subtle, cumulative effects on daily functioning.
Readiness for a full-dose psychedelic experience involves psychological stability, absence of contraindicated conditions, a clear therapeutic intention, a trusted qualified guide, and a safe setting. Full doses are not appropriate for everyone. No online resource can substitute for a proper clinical assessment. If you are managing active psychiatric conditions, always consult a specialist before considering either approach.
Safety, risks, and contraindications
This question must be answered by a qualified medical professional in the context of your specific medication, dose, and history. Generally, SSRIs are known to reduce the effects of classic psychedelics such as psilocybin by competing at serotonin receptors. Some protocols require tapering off SSRIs before a session, which carries its own risks. Combining MDMA with certain antidepressants including MAOIs can be medically dangerous. Never adjust psychiatric medication without professional supervision.
The interaction profile between psychedelics and psychiatric medications is complex and not fully understood. SSRIs can blunt the effects of psilocybin and LSD. Combining MDMA with MAOIs, tricyclic antidepressants, or lithium carries serious or potentially fatal risks including serotonin syndrome. Antipsychotics can block psychedelic effects. Mood stabilisers and benzodiazepines also interact in clinically significant ways. Any protocol involving psychiatric medication adjustments must be supervised by a qualified physician.
Psychedelic-assisted therapy is generally contraindicated for individuals with a personal or family history of psychotic disorders, including schizophrenia and bipolar disorder with psychotic features. Psychedelics can increase the risk of triggering latent psychotic episodes in susceptible individuals. This is a medical decision requiring careful assessment by a qualified psychiatrist. The overlap between the genetics of psychotic disorders and the populations most likely to seek psychedelic therapy makes this a critical and underresearched area.
Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder (HPPD) is a condition in which a person continues to experience visual disturbances, such as tracers, geometric patterns, or altered colour perception, long after the effects of a psychedelic have worn off. It is considered rare, but its true prevalence is unclear due to underreporting and limited research. Risk factors are not fully established. There is currently no approved treatment for HPPD, though some cases resolve over time.
A difficult psychedelic experience in a clinical setting may involve intense fear, paranoia, emotional overwhelm, confrontation with traumatic memories, or a sense of loss of self. Trained therapists use grounding techniques, reassurance, and calm presence rather than medication where possible. The quality of preparation, set and setting, and the therapeutic relationship are critical factors in whether a difficult experience becomes therapeutically useful or harmful. Benzodiazepines may be administered in cases of extreme distress or medical necessity.
In regulated settings, facilitators should be licensed through their jurisdiction's regulatory body, such as the Oregon Health Authority or hold authorised prescriber status in Australia. Indicators of legitimacy include formal training from accredited programmes, professional licensure in a related mental health field, transparent disclosure of training, willingness to provide references, and clear ethical agreements in writing. No current system provides comprehensive consumer protection in informal or underground settings.
Integration
Integration is the process of making meaning from and applying insights gained during a psychedelic experience to everyday life. It typically involves follow-up sessions with a therapist or integration coach, journaling, meditation, time in nature, and deliberate lifestyle changes. Integration is considered a critical component of therapeutic outcomes. Without adequate integration, even profound experiences may not translate into lasting change. The quality and duration of integration work is associated with better long-term outcomes in clinical studies.
Adverse integration outcomes do occur and are underreported in the literature. If you are experiencing worsened mental health, intrusive thoughts, dissociation, or emotional instability following a psychedelic experience, seek professional support promptly. Tell your healthcare provider what happened so they can provide appropriate care. Organisations including the Zendo Project and Fireside Project offer psychedelic crisis support. Do not attempt to manage a difficult post-experience period alone, especially if you are experiencing suicidal ideation or significant functional impairment.
Policy, ethics, and accountability
The FDA rejected Lykos Therapeutics' application for MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD in 2024, citing concerns about study design, functional unblinding, and questions about the generalisability of results. The decision does not mean the therapy is ineffective — it means further research with more rigorous methodology is required before regulatory approval. The decision has prompted broader discussion about how clinical trial frameworks designed for pharmaceutical drugs can or cannot accommodate complex psychedelic therapy interventions.
Sexual misconduct in psychedelic therapy settings is a documented and serious concern. Patients in altered states are intensely vulnerable, and the emotional intimacy associated particularly with MDMA creates conditions in which power imbalances can be exploited. Several cases of abuse have been reported in both underground and clinical settings. Responses have included ethical guidelines from training organisations, survivor advocacy work, and calls for mandatory reporting structures. Critics argue that existing safeguards remain insufficient and that the field has been slow to address this issue with the seriousness it deserves.
Legal liability for harm caused during psychedelic therapy is largely unresolved. In clinical trial settings, institutional review boards and sponsoring organisations carry responsibility. In regulated settings such as Oregon, licensed facilitators carry professional liability. In underground or informal settings, legal recourse for patients is extremely limited. The question of who bears accountability when severe adverse outcomes occur is one the field has not yet systematically addressed. Regulatory frameworks are evolving but remain incomplete.
Concerns about corporate capture of psychedelic medicine are widespread among researchers, advocates, and community members. As psychedelics move toward commercialisation, questions arise about patent protections on naturally occurring compounds, pricing strategies that limit access, influence over research agendas, and the risk of stripping therapeutic practices from their cultural and relational context. These concerns reflect genuine tensions between the grassroots origins of the psychedelic therapy movement and the requirements of the pharmaceutical and investment industries.
This is an emerging area of bioethical inquiry. Psychedelic experiences can lead to profound and sometimes lasting changes in personality, beliefs, values, and worldview. This raises the question of whether a person can meaningfully consent in advance to changes they cannot predict or fully understand. Standard informed consent frameworks may be inadequate for interventions with this profile. Researchers and ethicists are beginning to explore what enhanced or ongoing consent processes might look like in psychedelic therapy contexts, but there is no consensus position yet.
