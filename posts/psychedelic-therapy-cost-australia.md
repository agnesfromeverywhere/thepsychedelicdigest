Australia made history in July 2023. It became the first country in the world to legally allow psychiatrists to prescribe psilocybin and MDMA for therapeutic use.

Then reality arrived.

A full program — psychiatric assessment, preparation sessions, one or two supervised dosing sessions, and post-treatment integration — costs between $20,000 and $40,000 AUD. Medicare does not cover it. Private health insurance rarely does. Fewer than 100 psychiatrists in the entire country are authorised to prescribe it.

The result is a two-tier system. People with resources access world-first medicine. Everyone else either waits, goes without, or considers options that sit outside regulated frameworks entirely.

This article is for the second group. Not to push anyone toward anything unsafe. But because the underground is not the only alternative, and most people do not know what the legitimate options actually are.

## Why the Underground Is Not the Answer

Before the alternatives: a word on why going underground carries real risks that are worth understanding clearly.

Underground psychedelic therapy operates without screening. A competent therapist screens for cardiac conditions, medication interactions, personal and family history of psychosis, and trauma profiles that require specific preparation. Without that screening, the risk of a destabilising experience increases significantly.

It also operates without recourse. If something goes wrong, there is no professional body, no complaints process, no insurance, and no continuity of care.

The science that makes psychedelic therapy so compelling is inseparable from the clinical container that surrounds it. The drug is one part of the treatment. The preparation, the setting, the trained guides, and the integration support are the other parts. Remove those and you do not have a cheaper version of the therapy. You have a different thing entirely.

That said, legitimate lower-cost options do exist. Here are five of them.

## 1. Clinical Trials in Australia: Free, Medically Supervised, and Actively Recruiting

This is the most underutilised option in Australia and the most underreported.

Mind Medicine Australia, a non-profit, currently supplies medical-grade psilocybin and MDMA at no cost to clinical trials across the country. As of 2026, there are 25 active psychedelic trials in Australia, with more opening regularly.

Participating in a clinical trial means receiving the treatment in a fully supervised medical setting, with psychiatrists, therapists, and integration support included, at no cost to you. The trade-off is that you may receive a placebo in some trials, the treatment protocol is fixed rather than personalised, and eligibility criteria are strict.

To find active trials in Australia, visit [clinicaltrials.gov](https://clinicaltrials.gov) and search for psilocybin or MDMA with Australia as the location. Mind Medicine Australia also maintains a trial registry at [mindmedicineaustralia.org.au](https://mindmedicineaustralia.org.au).

## 2. Jamaica: Legal, Professional, and a Fraction of the Cost

Psilocybin has never been scheduled in Jamaica. It is fully legal, which means retreat operators there function in a genuinely permissive environment with no legal ambiguity.

Several medically-informed retreat centres operate in Jamaica with trained facilitators, preparation calls, integration support, and clinical screening. A full programme, including accommodation, typically costs between $3,000 and $6,000 USD, including flights from Australia.

This is not a loophole. It is a legitimate jurisdiction. The quality varies significantly between operators, so due diligence matters. Look for centres that screen participants, provide integration support, and have licensed mental health professionals on their team.

## 3. The Netherlands: Legal Truffle Therapy with Clinical Protocols

Psilocybin truffles are legal in the Netherlands. This has allowed a small but growing ecosystem of professional therapy centres to operate openly in Amsterdam and surrounds, many of them run by licensed psychologists and therapists.

Centres like Synthesis and Myco Meditations (which also operates in Jamaica) offer programmes that closely mirror clinical trial protocols: preparation sessions, supervised dosing in a clinical-style setting, and structured integration. Costs typically range from $2,500 to $5,000 EUR for a full programme, not including flights.

For Australians, combining this with a European trip makes the cost more palatable. It is also worth noting that the Netherlands' approach to quality control is more rigorous than many retreat destinations, given the legal clarity operators work within.

## 4. Oregon, USA: The First Regulated Psilocybin Service Model in the World

Oregon became the first US state to legalise psilocybin for therapeutic use in 2023. Licensed service centres began operating in 2024. Unlike clinical trials, Oregon's model does not require a diagnosis. Anyone can access a session with a licensed facilitator.

This is a meaningful distinction. Australia's legal access requires a diagnosis of treatment-resistant depression or PTSD. Oregon's model is open to anyone seeking a supervised psychedelic experience, regardless of clinical history.

Costs in Oregon currently range from $1,500 to $3,500 USD per session, including preparation and integration. For Australians willing to travel, this represents a legally regulated, professionally supervised option at roughly one-tenth of the Australian clinical cost.

The Oregon Health Authority maintains a public registry of licensed service centres at [psilocybinservices.health.oregon.gov](https://psilocybinservices.health.oregon.gov).

## 5. Ketamine Therapy in Australia: Legal, Medicare-Adjacent, and Available Now

This one surprises people.

Ketamine is a legal, Schedule 8 substance in Australia. Esketamine (Spravato), a ketamine derivative, received TGA approval and as of May 2025 has a PBS listing for treatment-resistant depression, meaning it is partially subsidised.

Ketamine is not the same as psilocybin or MDMA. The subjective experience is different and the mechanism of action is distinct. But the evidence base for ketamine-assisted therapy for treatment-resistant depression and PTSD is substantial, and it is the most accessible psychedelic-adjacent treatment currently available in Australia at a regulated clinical level.

Several ketamine clinics now operate across Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane, with session costs ranging from $500 to $1,500 AUD, significantly lower than psilocybin or MDMA programmes. With a PBS listing for esketamine, out-of-pocket costs reduce further for eligible patients.

It is not a perfect substitute. But for people who cannot access psilocybin or MDMA therapy legally in Australia right now, it is a clinically serious, legally accessible, and meaningfully cheaper option.

## The Bigger Picture

The access problem in Australian psychedelic therapy is not a secret. As of September 2025, only 87 patients had been treated with MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD and 47 with psilocybin-assisted therapy for treatment-resistant depression under the TGA's Authorised Prescriber program. In a country of 26 million people, that number speaks for itself.

The political will exists. The science is clear. The regulatory framework is in place.

What is missing is affordability, scale, and Medicare recognition.

Until those arrive, the five options above represent the most legitimate, safest, and most clinically serious paths available to Australians who cannot access or afford the domestic programme.

The underground is not the answer. But neither is simply waiting.
