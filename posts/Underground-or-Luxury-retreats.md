Six years of running a psychedelic therapy community on Instagram teaches you things no research paper will tell you.

I started the [@psychedelictherapyclub](https://www.instagram.com/psychedelictherapyclub) account in the years before mainstream media had decided psychedelics were interesting. Back then, you had to explain what psilocybin was. You had to clarify that no, this was not about getting high at festivals. I was also on TikTok before the ban cut that channel off. Together, those platforms gave me something rare: a direct, unfiltered window into how ordinary people relate to psychedelic healing. Not the people who can afford a week in Costa Rica. Not the people booking consultations with Sydney psychiatrists. Everyone else.

What I have observed over six years has crystallised into something I need to say plainly. The psychedelic healing space is splitting in two. On one side, a luxury industry is blooming, catering to people wealthy enough to buy transformation the same way they buy a business class seat. On the other, a quieter, less discussed movement is happening among people who cannot access formal therapy and are doing something I find genuinely remarkable. They are getting smarter. They are doing the work themselves, carefully, with more preparation and intentionality than most clinical trials would credit them for.

This is not an endorsement of underground use. It is an honest observation from someone who has spent six years listening.

## 1. The Price of Legal Access in Australia Is a Wall Most People Cannot Climb

Australia made history on 1 July 2023 when the [Therapeutic Goods Administration](https://www.tga.gov.au/products/unapproved-therapeutic-goods/mdma-and-psilocybine/access-pathways/mdma-and-psilocybine-hub) reclassified psilocybin and MDMA from Schedule 9 prohibited substances to Schedule 8 controlled drugs, making it the first country in the world to formally approve psychedelics for therapeutic use.

Then came the question nobody wanted to answer too loudly: how much does it cost?

A full course of psilocybin-assisted therapy in Australia [typically ranges between A$15,000 and A$30,000](https://www.psilocybin.com.au/faq), driven largely by the two-therapist safety model required under clinical guidelines, which demands many hours of intensive supervision across preparation, dosing, and integration sessions. Some practitioners have estimated the total cost closer to A$25,000 plus or minus A$10,000 for a multi-month program, [according to Monash University researchers](https://www.monash.edu/medicine/news/latest/2023-articles/the-tricky-economics-of-subsidising-psychedelics-for-mental-health-therapy). There is no Medicare rebate. No private health insurance coverage. No government subsidy path currently in place.

By September 2025, [only 87 patients had been treated with MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD, and 47 with psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression](https://psychedelicalpha.com/news/australias-psychedelic-experiment-progress-pitfalls-and-what-comes-next/), through an authorised prescriber scheme that could be counted in the dozens of participating psychiatrists. Nearly 200 patients in total had accessed the program since its launch, in a country of 26 million people where mental health presentations have been rising for years. Those numbers are not a revolution. They are a proof of concept available almost exclusively to those who can fund it.

[Mind Medicine Australia](https://mindmedicineaustralia.org.au/), which led the campaign for TGA rescheduling, has begun a Patient Support Fund to partially subsidise costs. The Australian Department of Veterans' Affairs has announced a $740 million rehabilitation plan that includes funding for MDMA and psilocybin treatment for eligible veterans. These are meaningful steps. But for the vast majority of Australians living with treatment-resistant depression or PTSD, the clinical pathway remains out of reach.

## 2. The Luxury Retreat Market Is Booming, and It Is Selling Something Real

At the opposite end of the financial spectrum, the global luxury psychedelic retreat sector has become one of the more quietly remarkable corners of wellness tourism.

Psychedelic retreats, once associated with budget travellers willing to rough it in Peruvian jungles, have gone upscale. [A 2023 study counted 298 retreat locations operating globally](https://www.threemagazine.com/the-allure-of-luxury-psychedelic-retreats/), though many more likely exist without advertising online. Some charge fees exceeding US$15,000 for a week. The overall psychedelic drugs market is projected to reach US$8.33 billion by 2032. Wellness tourism as a whole is expected to grow to US$1.4 trillion by 2027.

In Jamaica, where psilocybin is legal, [MycoMeditations](https://mycomeditations.com/) has guided more than 2,000 guests through over 6,000 psilocybin sessions. In Costa Rica, [Rythmia](https://www.rythmia.com/) has served more than 17,000 guests since 2016. In the Netherlands, [Synthesis Institute](https://www.synthesisinstitute.com/immersion-retreats) offers legal psilocybin truffle retreats with licensed therapists. These operations are not operating on goodwill alone. They have the resources to conduct rigorous pre-screening, maintain small group sizes, hire licensed clinicians alongside traditional facilitators, and provide structured integration support.

I want to be careful here, because it would be easy to dismiss this as wealthy indulgence. The best luxury retreats hold their containers with genuine care. The problem is not that they exist. The problem is what their existence implies about who gets access to healing.

## 3. Alcohol Has Become the Drug People Are Running From

This is perhaps the most significant shift I have noticed in six years of community observation, and it deserves more attention than it receives.

For most of the 20th century, the cultural script placed psychedelics and alcohol in the same moral category, or treated psychedelics as the greater concern. Fear of psychosis. Flashbacks. Losing your mind. That conditioning ran deep, and in the early years of my community I felt it constantly. People were ashamed to admit their curiosity.

That script has been rewritten, and I do not believe it is going back.

The people I speak with now distinguish very clearly between alcohol, which they increasingly see as physiologically dangerous, socially coercive, and unfairly normalised, and psychedelics, which they view as non-addictive, potentially therapeutic, and unjustly stigmatised. Alcohol, for many in this community, has become the drug they are actively working to reduce or eliminate.

This is not anecdote alone. A landmark 2022 randomised controlled trial [published in JAMA Psychiatry](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9403854/) found that psilocybin-assisted psychotherapy significantly reduced heavy drinking days compared to placebo in adults with alcohol use disorder. The University of Sydney is currently running [a Phase 2 trial of psilocybin-assisted therapy for AUD in Australia](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06444243). The therapeutic logic my community has arrived at independently is coherent, and science is beginning to catch up with it.

## 4. Six Years of Community Observation: What Has Actually Changed

When I started my account in 2019, the questions I received were often defensive. People wanted reassurance that psychedelics were not dangerous. There was shame in the asking, and considerable caution.

That has changed, completely, and quite fast.

The shift became visible around 2021 and accelerated after Australia's TGA announcement in 2023. People now arrive in the community with fundamentally different questions. They are not asking whether psychedelics work. They know they work, or at least believe they do, because the science has caught up with what [decades of clinical research](https://bpspubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bph.17398) and indigenous traditions have long affirmed. The question now is how. How do I prepare? What does a difficult experience feel like and how do I navigate it? What does integration mean in practice, day to day?

The people asking these questions are not reckless. Many have tried conventional treatment for years, sometimes decades, and found it insufficient. They cannot afford A$30,000. They cannot fly to Costa Rica. They live in regional New South Wales or outer Melbourne or rural Queensland, where the phrase "authorised psychiatrist" sounds like something from another country. And so they have become, by necessity, highly informed.

## 5. The Underground Is Getting Smarter, Not More Reckless

I want to be precise about what I am observing, because it is easy to misread.

I am not saying that informal psychedelic use is equivalent to supervised clinical therapy, or that it is without risk. I am saying that the people engaging with it outside formal systems are, on average, significantly more informed than the public narrative gives them credit for.

Six years ago, preparation conversations in my community were minimal. People shared experiences after the fact. There was little discussion of dosage, contraindications, set and setting, or integration planning.

Today, those conversations happen before the experience. People discuss their mental health history openly. They ask about medication interactions. They research [set and setting](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/glossary) with the same care that clinical protocols prescribe. They prepare for difficult experiences rather than hoping to avoid them. They plan for the days that follow, understanding that integration is where the real therapeutic work begins.

Many have read published research. Some have started with microdosing, using sub-perceptual doses to understand their own neurological baseline before considering deeper work. I wrote a practical guide to that process in [The Microdosing Guidebook](https://www.amazon.com.au/dp/0645763209), which has become a reference point for many people in this community approaching psychedelics for the first time.

The democratisation of psychedelic knowledge has not produced the wave of harm that early gatekeepers feared. It has produced a cohort of people applying therapeutic principles to their own experiences with considerable seriousness. This does not eliminate risk. A trained therapist present in a difficult moment matters enormously. But the portrait of uninformed, reckless users is, in my observation, increasingly inaccurate.

## 6. The Industry Is Building Beautiful Containers for the Wrong People

The luxury retreat industry and the TGA-approved clinical pathway have something important in common: neither is meaningfully addressing access for the majority.

[Regulatory barriers in Australia are substantial](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00048674251398677). Psychiatrists seeking Authorised Prescriber status must obtain approval from a Human Research Ethics Committee registered with the National Health and Medical Research Council, then seek separate authorisation from the TGA, while demonstrating training, competency, and robust protocols. As of 2025, access is restricted to PTSD and treatment-resistant depression only. Most clinics rely on imported pharmaceutical-grade psilocybin requiring both Office of Drug Control permits and TGA exemptions. There are no cost-effectiveness data published for psilocybin, and [only three studies on MDMA cost-effectiveness exist](https://www.monash.edu/medicine/news/latest/2023-articles/the-tricky-economics-of-subsidising-psychedelics-for-mental-health-therapy), making public subsidy approval structurally years away.

The luxury retreat sector, meanwhile, is building ever more beautiful containers for people who can fund US$15,000 wellness holidays. Both worlds are, in their own way, excellent at what they do. Both are structurally available to a narrow slice of the population.

## 7. The People Without the Money Are Leading the Field in Ways It Cannot Yet Acknowledge

I have spent six years watching a community evolve from shame and confusion to clarity and sophistication. The substance of that evolution matters.

The people who cannot afford the retreat and cannot access the clinic are not abandoning healing. They are developing their own protocols, their own harm reduction frameworks, their own integration support networks. They are sharing knowledge with the rigour and care that clinical researchers aspire to. They are, in many ways, running a distributed, uncontrolled, highly imperfect but deeply human version of the study that formal medicine cannot yet afford to run at scale.

This is not a comfortable observation for an industry with significant financial interests in being the gatekeepers of healing. But it is what I see.

Psychedelic therapy does not belong only to those who can pay for a week in Jamaica or find an authorised psychiatrist in Sydney. The plants were here before the retreats. The knowledge was held in communities long before clinical trials began. The people without the money have not lost their right to heal. They have had to become more creative about it.

What the access gap requires is policy change, public funding, and an honest reckoning with who the psychedelic industry is building itself for. The gap between the luxury market and the community I see every day in my comments section is enormous, and it is growing. That community is, in many ways, leading. The field just has not found the language to say so yet.
