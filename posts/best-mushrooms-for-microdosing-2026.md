What mushrooms are used for microdosing? The answer splits into two distinct categories. Psilocybin-containing mushrooms are used for their neuroplasticity and mood effects. Functional mushrooms like lion's mane are used for cognitive support and neurogenesis. Many practitioners combine them deliberately. This guide covers both clearly, including what to choose, what to avoid, and why the difference matters.

## Psilocybin-Containing Mushrooms

Psilocybin mushrooms contain two primary active compounds: psilocybin, which the body converts to psilocin, and psilocin itself, which acts on serotonin 2A receptors to produce both the full psychedelic effect at high doses and the subtle sub-perceptual shifts associated with microdosing.

The most widely used species is Psilocybe cubensis. It is the most commonly cultivated, most consistently available, and most studied psilocybin mushroom in the world. Potency varies significantly between strains, which is why precision weighing to 0.01g is non-negotiable. Two samples of the same species can have meaningfully different psilocybin concentrations depending on growing conditions, substrate, and drying method.

Other psilocybin-containing species include Psilocybe semilanceata, commonly called liberty caps, which grow wild in temperate grasslands across Europe, New Zealand, and parts of Australia. Liberty caps are generally more potent gram-for-gram than Psilocybe cubensis and should be dosed conservatively. Psilocybe azurescens and Psilocybe cyanescens are wood-loving species with very high psilocybin content and are not recommended for beginners due to their potency and the difficulty of consistent dosing.

The [Psilocybin Alpha database](https://psilocybinalpha.com) maintains a useful reference on species identification and potency ranges that is worth bookmarking if you want to go deeper on the taxonomy.

## The Lion's Mane Question

Lion's mane (Hericium erinaceus) is a functional mushroom, not a psychedelic one. It produces no altered states and no hallucinogenic effect at any dose. What it does contain are compounds called hericenones and erinacines, which have been shown in both animal studies and early human trials to stimulate nerve growth factor production, a key driver of neurogenesis.

Lion's mane became central to the microdosing conversation through the Stamets Stack, a protocol developed by mycologist Paul Stamets that combines a small psilocybin dose with lion's mane and niacin (vitamin B3), taken on a four-days-on, three-days-off schedule. The rationale is that the neuroplasticity window created by psilocybin is enhanced by lion's mane's nerve growth factor stimulation, while niacin acts as a peripheral vasodilator to improve delivery. A 2021 observational study in [eLife](https://elifesciences.org) found that people using the Stamets Stack reported greater improvements in mood and wellbeing than those using psilocybin alone, though the data was self-reported and non-controlled.

Lion's mane is legal everywhere, widely available in capsule and powder form, carries minimal risk, and can be taken daily independently of any psilocybin protocol.

## Other Functional Mushrooms Worth Knowing

Beyond lion's mane, several functional mushrooms appear regularly within the microdosing community as complementary support. Reishi (Ganoderma lucidum) is widely used for its adaptogenic and stress-regulating properties and pairs well with microdosing cycles that can occasionally produce mild anxiety. Cordyceps is used for energy, endurance, and adrenal support, particularly on non-dose days when some people notice a subtle energy dip.

Neither produces psychedelic effects. Both are well-researched and available without restriction. [Paul Stamets' work at Fungi Perfecti](https://fungi.com) is a good starting point if you want evidence-based guidance on functional mushroom supplementation.

## What to Avoid

Amanita muscaria, the red-and-white fly agaric, is psychoactive but not psilocybin-containing. Its active compounds are muscimol and ibotenic acid, which act on GABA receptors rather than serotonin receptors. The experience is qualitatively different, considerably less predictable at low doses, and the preparation method significantly affects both potency and safety. It is not a standard microdosing substance and should not be treated as a psilocybin alternative.

Wild foraging for psychoactive mushrooms carries serious safety risks due to the possibility of misidentification. Several toxic species closely resemble psilocybin-containing ones. Unless you are an expert mycologist with extensive field identification experience, foraging for psychoactive mushrooms is not recommended.

## Practical Summary

For psilocybin microdosing: Psilocybe cubensis is the standard starting point. Weigh every dose precisely. Start at 0.1g and calibrate from there.

For cognitive and neuroplasticity support: lion's mane is the most evidence-backed functional mushroom available, legal, safe, and increasingly well-studied.
