What mushrooms are used for microdosing? The answer depends on what you are trying to achieve. Psilocybin-containing mushrooms are used for their psychedelic and neuroplasticity effects. Functional mushrooms like lion's mane are used for cognitive support and neurogenesis. Many practitioners combine them. This guide covers both categories clearly.

## Psilocybin-Containing Mushrooms

Psilocybin mushrooms are the most commonly used substances in microdosing practice globally. They contain two primary active compounds: psilocybin, which the body converts to psilocin, and psilocin, which acts on serotonin 2A receptors to produce both the full psychedelic effect at high doses and the subtle sub-perceptual effects associated with microdosing.

The most widely used species by microdosers is Psilocybe cubensis. It is the most commonly cultivated, most consistently available, and most studied psilocybin-containing mushroom. Potency varies significantly between strains of Psilocybe cubensis, which is one reason precision weighing to 0.01g is so important. Two samples of the same species can have meaningfully different psilocybin concentrations depending on growing conditions and drying method.

Other psilocybin-containing species include Psilocybe semilanceata (liberty caps), which are found growing wild in temperate grasslands in Europe, New Zealand, and parts of Australia. Liberty caps are generally considered more potent gram-for-gram than Psilocybe cubensis. Psilocybe azurescens and Psilocybe cyanescens are wood-loving species with very high psilocybin content and are not recommended for beginners due to their potency and higher variability.

## Key Considerations for Psilocybin Mushroom Selection

When choosing a psilocybin mushroom for microdosing, consistency and accurate dosing are the priority. Psilocybe cubensis is the standard choice because it is the most well-characterised, the most available in communities where cultivation is practiced, and the one with the largest body of user data behind it.

Potency calibration takes time regardless of species. Start at 0.1g of dried mushroom, observe, and adjust. Do not assume a dose that worked for someone else will work the same way for you. Individual sensitivity to psilocybin varies significantly.

## The Role of Lion's Mane

Lion's mane (Hericium erinaceus) is a functional mushroom, not a psychedelic one. It contains compounds called hericenones and erinacines that have been shown in animal studies and some human trials to stimulate nerve growth factor (NGF) production, which is associated with neurogenesis. Lion's mane produces no psychedelic effect.

In the context of microdosing, lion's mane became widely discussed through the Stamets Stack protocol developed by mycologist Paul Stamets. The stack combines a small dose of psilocybin mushrooms with lion's mane and niacin (vitamin B3), taken four days on and three days off. The theory is that the neuroplasticity window opened by psilocybin is amplified by lion's mane's NGF-stimulating effects, and that niacin acts as a peripheral delivery enhancer.

A 2021 observational study published in eLife found that people using the Stamets Stack reported greater improvements in mood and wellbeing than those using psilocybin alone, though this was self-reported data from non-controlled conditions.

Lion's mane is legal everywhere, widely available, and carries minimal risk. It can be taken daily regardless of whether you are on a psilocybin protocol and is increasingly used as a standalone cognitive support supplement.

## Other Functional Mushrooms Commonly Used Alongside Microdosing

Beyond lion's mane, several other functional mushrooms are popular within the microdosing community as complementary support. Reishi (Ganoderma lucidum) is used for its adaptogenic and stress-modulating properties. Cordyceps is used for energy, endurance, and adrenal support. Neither produces psychedelic effects.

Several community members at @psychedelictherapyclub stack reishi and lion's mane together on both dose and non-dose days as a background neurological support protocol, independent of their psilocybin microdosing cycle.

## What to Avoid

Amanita muscaria, sometimes called fly agaric, is a psychoactive mushroom but not a psilocybin-containing one. Its active compounds are muscimol and ibotenic acid, which work on GABA receptors rather than serotonin receptors. The user experience is qualitatively different, less predictable at low doses, and the drying and preparation method significantly affects potency and safety profile. It is not a standard microdosing substance, and comparing it to psilocybin mushrooms is misleading.

Wild mushroom foraging for psychoactive species carries significant safety risks due to the possibility of misidentification. Several toxic species closely resemble psilocybin-containing ones. If you are not an expert mycologist, foraging for psychoactive mushrooms is not recommended.

For psilocybin microdosing: Psilocybe cubensis is the standard starting point. Weigh precisely. Start low.

For cognitive and neuroplasticity support: lion's mane is the most evidence-backed functional mushroom available without any legal or safety complications.
