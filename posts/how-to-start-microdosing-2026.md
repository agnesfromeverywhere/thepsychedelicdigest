If you want to start microdosing, here is the short version: choose a protocol, get a precision scale that measures to 0.01g, start at 0.1g of dried psilocybin mushrooms, take it in the morning, and write down everything you notice. That is the foundation. Everything below will help you do it properly.

Microdosing has moved from a fringe experiment to a mainstream conversation over the past decade. After six years running the Psychedelic Therapy Club community at [@psychedelictherapyclub](https://www.instagram.com/psychedelictherapyclub), I have watched thousands of people start this practice, and I have seen clearly where beginners go wrong. This guide exists to help you avoid those mistakes.

## What Is Microdosing?

Microdosing is the practice of taking a sub-perceptual dose of a psychedelic substance, typically between one-tenth and one-twentieth of a full recreational dose. The goal is not to trip. A correctly calibrated microdose produces no hallucinations, no perceptual impairment, and no loss of function. Most people describe a subtle shift in mood, focus, or creative thinking, or nothing immediately noticeable at all.

The most widely used substances are psilocybin mushrooms, LSD, and to a lesser extent mescaline-containing cacti. For definitions of key terms including sub-perceptual, psilocybin, and protocol, visit the [Psychedelic Digest glossary](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/glossary).

## How Much Is a Microdose?

For dried psilocybin mushrooms, a standard starting microdose is 0.1g. Most people calibrate to between 0.1g and 0.3g over the first few weeks. For LSD, the range is typically 5 to 15 micrograms. These are starting points, not targets. Body weight, individual neurochemistry, and the potency of your specific batch all affect your experience.

The most common beginner mistake is starting too high. If you feel anything clearly perceptible within the first hour, your dose is too large. A true microdose should feel, at most, like a very good morning.

## Which Protocol Should You Follow?

The most well-documented starting point is the Fadiman Protocol: one day on, two days off, repeated over four to eight weeks. It was developed by psychedelic researcher Dr James Fadiman and has the largest body of self-reported data behind it. For a full breakdown of how it works and why the off days matter, read the [Fadiman Protocol guide](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/blog/the-fadiman-protocol-explained-2026) on this site.

Other options include the Stamets Stack, which combines psilocybin with lion's mane mushroom and niacin on a four-days-on, three-days-off schedule, and the Every Other Day protocol. Each suits different people for different reasons.

## What Do You Need Before You Start?

Four things. First, a precision scale measuring to 0.01g. Dried mushrooms vary significantly in potency and eyeballing is not accurate enough. Second, a journal. Your data is only useful if you record it consistently. Third, a protocol you have chosen deliberately. Fourth, an honest look at your mental health history. Microdosing is not recommended for people with a personal or family history of psychosis, schizophrenia, or bipolar I disorder. If you are currently taking SSRIs or MAOIs, speak with a doctor before starting. The [Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS)](https://maps.org) publishes guidance on contraindications that is worth reading.

## What to Expect in the First Two Weeks

Week one is calibration. Many people feel nothing on their first dose. Others notice mild energy, improved mood, or slight restlessness. A small number discover their starting dose is too high and need to step down.

Week two is where patterns begin to emerge. Some people find dose days energising and productive. Others find that the off days, particularly the day immediately after a dose, are their best days. Neither experience is wrong. Both are useful data.

## What the Research Shows

The clinical research on microdosing is growing but not yet conclusive. A 2021 study published in [eLife](https://elifesciences.org) by Vince Polito and Frederick Barrett analysed self-reports from 953 microdosers and found improvements in mood, focus, and psychological wellbeing. A 2022 self-blinding study from [Imperial College London](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/brain-sciences/research/psychedelics/) found positive outcomes in both active and placebo groups, highlighting the role of expectation in the experience. The honest position is that the signal is promising but individual variation is high.

For a deeper look at the evidence, read the [Microdosing and Mental Health](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/blog/microdosing-and-mental-health-research-2026) guide on this site.

## The One Thing Most Guides Leave Out

Microdosing amplifies your baseline. If you are moving through a period of grief, high stress, or emotional instability, a microdose will not smooth that over. It will bring it into sharper relief. This is not necessarily bad, but it requires that you are prepared for it and have support around you. Approach this practice with intention, not as a shortcut.
