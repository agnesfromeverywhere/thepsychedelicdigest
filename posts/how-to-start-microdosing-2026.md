How do you start microdosing? You start with a sub-perceptual dose of a psychedelic substance, typically psilocybin mushrooms or LSD, taken on a structured schedule over several weeks. You track your experience, adjust your dose, and approach the process with intention. That is the short answer. Here is everything else you need to know.

Microdosing has moved from fringe experiment to mainstream curiosity over the past decade, and the Psychedelic Therapy Club community at @psychedelictherapyclub has been at the centre of that shift since 2019. Six years of conversations, questions, and shared experiences have given me a clear picture of where beginners go wrong, and how to do this well. This guide covers the foundations.

## What Is Microdosing?

Microdosing is the practice of taking a very small, sub-perceptual dose of a psychedelic substance, typically between one-tenth and one-twentieth of a full recreational dose. The goal is not to trip. A properly calibrated microdose should produce no hallucinations, minimal perceptual changes, and no impairment. Most people describe a subtle shift in mood, focus, or creativity, or nothing noticeable at all on dose days.

The most commonly used substances are psilocybin mushrooms (Psilocybe cubensis and related species), LSD or its analogues, and to a lesser extent mescaline-containing cacti. For a full glossary of terms, visit [thepsychedelicdigest.com/glossary](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/glossary).

## How Much Is a Microdose?

For dried psilocybin mushrooms, a typical starting microdose is 0.1g, with most people settling between 0.1g and 0.3g after calibration. For LSD, a microdose is typically 5 to 15 micrograms. These ranges are starting points. Body weight, sensitivity, and individual neurochemistry mean the right dose varies from person to person.

The single most common beginner mistake is starting too high. If you feel anything perceptible within the first hour, your dose is too large. A true microdose should feel, at most, like a very good morning.

## Which Protocol Should You Follow?

The most widely used and researched microdosing schedule is the Fadiman Protocol, developed by psychedelic researcher Dr James Fadiman. It involves dosing one day on, two days off, repeated over four to eight weeks. This spacing allows the substance to clear your system before the next dose and prevents tolerance buildup.

Other popular schedules include the Stamets Stack (four days on, three days off, combined with lion's mane mushroom and niacin) and the Every Other Day protocol. For a full breakdown, see the article on [The Fadiman Protocol](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/blog) on this site.

## What Do You Need Before You Start?

Before your first dose, you need four things. First, a reliable, accurately weighed supply. Dried mushrooms vary in potency, so a precision scale measuring to 0.01g is non-negotiable. Second, a journal to track your experience. Third, a protocol you have chosen deliberately, not impulsively. Fourth, an honest assessment of your mental health history. Microdosing is not recommended for people with a personal or family history of psychosis, schizophrenia, or bipolar I disorder. If in doubt, speak with a healthcare professional first.

## What to Expect in the First Two Weeks

Week one is calibration. Many people feel nothing on their first dose. Others notice mild energy, improved mood, or slight restlessness. A small number discover their starting dose is too high and need to step down.

Week two tends to be where people begin to see patterns. Some find dose days energising. Others prefer non-dose days, reporting that the integration period is where the real shift happens. Neither experience is wrong.

## What the Research Tells Us

The clinical research on microdosing is still young. A 2021 study published in eLife found that self-reported microdosers reported improved mood, focus, and reduced anxiety, though placebo effects were significant. A 2022 Imperial College London study found positive mood effects at one month but cautioned that blinding was difficult in self-selected samples. The honest position is that the evidence is promising but not yet conclusive. Most of what we know comes from community observation and self-reporting, which is valuable, but not the same as controlled trials.

## The One Thing Most Guides Don't Tell You

Microdosing amplifies your baseline. If you are going through a period of high stress, grief, or instability, a microdose will not smooth that over. It will bring it into sharper focus. This is not a problem if you are prepared for it and have support around you. It is a problem if you are looking for an escape. Set your intentions clearly before you begin.
