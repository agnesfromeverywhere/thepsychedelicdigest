Is microdosing legal in Australia in 2026? For most Australians, the answer is still no. Possessing or using psilocybin mushrooms for personal use remains illegal in every Australian state and territory except in very limited, tightly regulated therapeutic contexts. However, the legal landscape has shifted significantly since 2023 and is continuing to evolve. Here is a clear, current overview.

## What Happened in 2023

In February 2023, the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) made a landmark decision to reschedule psilocybin and MDMA. From 1 July 2023, psilocybin moved from Schedule 9 (Prohibited Substance) to Schedule 8 (Controlled Drug) for use in specific therapeutic settings. This change made Australia one of the first countries in the world to allow these substances within a regulated medical framework.

What this means in practice: authorised psychiatrists can now legally prescribe psilocybin to patients with treatment-resistant depression under the Special Access Scheme. This is a clinical, supervised context, not a personal use framework.

## What It Does Not Mean

The 2023 rescheduling does not legalise microdosing for personal use. Possessing psilocybin mushrooms without a prescription remains illegal. Growing them remains illegal. Purchasing them outside of an authorised channel remains illegal. The legal pathway created in 2023 is narrow, expensive, and requires a formal psychiatric diagnosis and authorised prescriber.

For most Australians who are microdosing, the activity sits in a legally grey-to-illegal space depending on jurisdiction. State laws govern possession offences, and these vary. The ACT decriminalised small amounts of personal drugs in 2023, which means possession of small quantities of psilocybin mushrooms there carries a civil rather than criminal penalty. No other state or territory has followed.

## State-by-State Overview

In New South Wales, Queensland, Victoria, Western Australia, South Australia, Tasmania, and the Northern Territory, possession of psilocybin mushrooms remains a criminal offence. Penalties vary by state and by quantity, ranging from fines and diversion to potential imprisonment for larger quantities.

In the ACT, possession of up to 0.25g of psilocybin (the active compound) for personal use is a civil infringement, not a criminal offence, since 2023. This is a meaningful harm reduction step, but not decriminalisation of cultivation or supply.

## The Therapeutic Access Pathway

If you are living with treatment-resistant depression and have not responded to at least two lines of conventional antidepressants, you may be eligible to access psilocybin-assisted therapy through an authorised psychiatrist under the Special Access Scheme Category B pathway. This is a legitimate, legal option that exists in 2026, though access is limited by the number of authorised prescribers and the cost of treatment.

This is not the same as microdosing. Therapeutic psilocybin sessions use full doses in a supervised clinical context. Microdosing is not currently part of any approved therapeutic protocol in Australia.

## What This Means for You

If you are microdosing in Australia outside of the ACT, you are doing so outside the law. The risk of prosecution is low for personal use quantities in most states, but it is not zero. Quantity thresholds matter, and anything that could be interpreted as supply, including sharing with friends, carries a significantly higher legal risk.

## Where the Law Is Heading

The political momentum in Australia is broadly in the direction of reform. Clinical trial data from Australian institutions continues to build a positive evidence base. Advocacy organisations have been instrumental in driving the 2023 TGA changes and continue to push for broader access.

It is plausible that the next five years will see further decriminalisation or expanded therapeutic access frameworks in Australia. Several state governments have indicated interest in harm reduction policy reviews. But in 2026, personal use microdosing remains illegal in most of the country.
