A microdosing journal is not optional. It is the single most important tool you have for understanding whether microdosing is working for you, how to calibrate your dose, and what patterns emerge over time. Without it, you are simply taking a substance with no way to distinguish its effects from placebo, from your ordinary mood variability, or from other changes in your life. Here is exactly what to track and how to build a system that is actually useful.

## Why Journalling Matters More Than Most People Realise

The body of clinical research on microdosing is still developing, and much of what we understand comes from self-reported data, including the large observational studies by Vince Polito, James Fadiman, and others. Your journal is your contribution to your own data set. Approached seriously, it will tell you things about your patterns, sensitivities, and rhythms that no external guide can tell you.

It also provides accountability. The ritual of recording forces you to pause and actually notice what is happening, rather than drifting through a protocol on autopilot.

## The 7 Things to Track

The first thing to track is whether it is a dose day or a non-dose day. This sounds obvious, but consistently logging it ensures your other data points are always in context. Mark D for dose day and ND for non-dose day at the top of every entry.

The second is dose amount. Record exactly how much you took in grams (for dried mushrooms) or micrograms (for LSD). If you changed your dose from a previous session, note the previous amount too and why you changed it.

The third is mood and emotional tone, recorded on a simple scale and in qualitative notes. A 1 to 10 scale for overall mood plus two or three sentences describing your emotional texture is sufficient. Note anything specific: irritability, warmth, flatness, openness, sadness. Be honest. Positive bias in self-reporting is the most common data error.

The fourth is energy and physical sensations. Note your energy level on the same 1 to 10 scale. Also note any physical sensations: appetite changes, sleep quality the previous night, tension or ease in your body, any nausea or headache.

The fifth is focus and cognitive quality. Rate your ability to sustain attention and engage with complex tasks. Note specifically what kind of work you did and how it felt. Did you start tasks easily or with friction? Did you go deep or skim? Were you more creative or more analytical than usual?

The sixth is notable events or external factors. If you had a difficult conversation, a bad night of sleep, received important news, or drank alcohol the day before, record it. These variables affect your experience and without noting them, your data will be misleading. The goal is to isolate the substance's effect as cleanly as possible from everything else happening in your life.

The seventh is anything unexpected. Unusual emotional reactions, strong memories surfacing, dreams with unusual vividness, moments of unexpected insight or creativity. These are often the most interesting data points.

## When to Write Each Entry

Write in the morning, within an hour of waking, before you have looked at your phone or engaged with the day. This captures your baseline state before external inputs shape it. If you took a dose, record it at this point.

Write a second brief entry in the evening, no more than five minutes. Note how the day went, any shifts in your afternoon or evening energy, and your sleep intention. This gives you a before-and-after comparison for each dose day that morning-only journalling cannot provide.

## How to Make Sense of Your Data Over Time

At the end of each week, review all seven entries. Look for patterns. Are dose days consistently different from non-dose days? In what direction? Is day two of the Fadiman Protocol (the transition day) consistently better, worse, or no different from day one? Is your mood baseline shifting upward, downward, or remaining flat across the protocol?

At the end of a full cycle, usually four to eight weeks, do a full review. Write a protocol summary: what worked, what did not, what you want to change in the next cycle, and whether you noticed any changes that persisted through non-dose days and break periods.

## Format Options

A physical notebook works well for morning journalling. A structured digital template in Notion or a simple Google Sheet works well for tracking numerical scores and reviewing patterns visually over time. Several people in the @psychedelictherapyclub community use both: a physical journal for qualitative reflection and a digital tracker for quantitative scores.

Whatever format you choose, consistency matters more than sophistication. A simple notebook used every day outperforms a beautiful Notion template used three times a week.
