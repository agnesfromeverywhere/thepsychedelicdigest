A microdosing journal is not optional. It is the single most important tool you have for understanding whether microdosing is actually working for you, how to calibrate your dose, and what genuine patterns emerge over time. Without it, you are taking a substance with no reliable way to distinguish its effects from ordinary mood variation, placebo, or the dozen other variables that shape how you feel on any given day.

Here is exactly what to track, when to track it, and how to make sense of what you collect.

## Why Journalling Matters More Than Most People Realise

Much of what we know about microdosing comes from self-reported data, including the major observational studies by [Vince Polito at Macquarie University](https://researchers.mq.edu.au/en/persons/vince-polito), James Fadiman's long-running participant research, and the Imperial College London self-blinding study. Your journal is your version of that data. Approached seriously, it will surface patterns about your own rhythms, sensitivities, and responses that no external guide can tell you.

Journalling also builds accountability. The ritual of recording forces you to pause and actually notice what is happening, rather than drifting through a protocol on autopilot and deciding at the end of eight weeks that it either worked or it did not without being able to say why.

## The 7 Things to Track

**1. Dose day or non-dose day.** Mark D or ND at the top of every entry. Every other data point needs this context to be interpretable.

**2. Dose amount.** Record exactly how much you took in grams or micrograms. If you adjusted from a previous session, note the previous amount and why you changed it.

**3. Mood and emotional tone.** Use a 1 to 10 scale plus two or three sentences of qualitative description. Note specifics: irritability, warmth, flatness, openness, anxiety, sadness. Positive bias in self-reporting is the most common data error in microdosing journals. Be honest, even when the entry is unflattering.

**4. Energy and physical sensations.** Rate your energy on the same 1 to 10 scale. Note appetite changes, sleep quality from the previous night, tension or ease in your body, any nausea or headache. Physical data is as important as psychological data.

**5. Focus and cognitive quality.** Rate your ability to sustain attention and engage with complex work. Note what kind of work you did and how it felt. Did tasks start easily or with friction? Did you go deep or skim? Were you more generative or more analytical than usual?

**6. External variables.** If you had a difficult conversation, a poor night of sleep, received significant news, consumed alcohol the previous day, or anything else that could affect your state, record it. The goal is to isolate the substance's effect as cleanly as possible. Variables you do not record cannot be accounted for.

**7. Unexpected observations.** Unusual emotional reactions, strong memories surfacing, unusually vivid dreams, moments of unexpected insight or creative connection. These are often the most informative entries in the entire journal.

## When to Write

Write in the morning, within an hour of waking, before you have looked at your phone. This captures your baseline before external inputs shape your state. If it is a dose day, record that you have dosed or are about to dose at this point.

Write a second brief entry in the evening, five minutes maximum. Note how the day developed, any shifts in your afternoon or evening energy, and how you are sleeping. This gives you a genuine before-and-after comparison that morning-only journalling cannot provide.

## How to Make Sense of Your Data

At the end of each week, read back through all seven entries and look for patterns. Are dose days consistently different from non-dose days? In which direction? Is day two of the [Fadiman Protocol](https://thepsychedelicdigest.com/blog/the-fadiman-protocol-explained-2026), the transition day, consistently your best or your hardest? Is your overall mood baseline shifting as the protocol progresses?

At the end of a full cycle, write a protocol summary: what worked, what did not, what you want to adjust in the next cycle, and whether you noticed any changes that persisted through non-dose days and your break period. This summary becomes the foundation for your next cycle.

## Format Options

A physical notebook works well for morning journalling because it removes the temptation to check your phone immediately after writing. A structured digital template in Notion or a simple Google Sheet works well for tracking numerical scores and reviewing patterns across weeks. Several people in the community use both: a physical journal for qualitative reflection and a digital tracker for quantitative scores that can be graphed easily.

Whatever format you choose, consistency outperforms sophistication. A plain notebook used every day produces better data than a beautifully designed Notion template used three times a week.
