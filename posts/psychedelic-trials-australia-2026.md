## Why Australia Is Leading in Psychedelic Research

When the TGA rescheduled psilocybin and MDMA from Schedule 9 to Schedule 8 in February 2023, it did more than open a clinical pathway for patients. It removed significant bureaucratic barriers for researchers.

Before rescheduling, Australian scientists studying psychedelics faced the same institutional hurdles as researchers in most countries: lengthy ethics processes, difficulty sourcing pharmaceutical-grade compounds, and the reputational risk of working with prohibited substances.

After rescheduling, those barriers dropped. **Australia became one of the most permissive environments in the world for psychedelic research**, and the scientific community moved quickly.

The result is a trial landscape in 2026 that is more active than most Australians realise, and more varied than most media coverage suggests.

## How Many Trials Are Active in 2026?

As of mid-2026, there are approximately 25 active or actively recruiting psychedelic clinical trials in Australia, according to data from the Australian New Zealand Clinical Trials Registry and clinicaltrials.gov.

This represents a significant increase from the five trials active in early 2023. The pipeline continues to grow, with several trials currently in ethics review expected to begin recruiting in late 2026.

**The Australian National University** is running a national outcomes registry that collects real-world data from all patients treated under the Authorised Prescriber scheme, adding a parallel real-world evidence track alongside formal trials.

## What Substances Are Being Trialled?

**Psilocybin** is the most widely studied substance in the current Australian trial landscape, appearing in trials across depression, anxiety, eating disorders, and end-of-life distress.

**MDMA** is being studied primarily for PTSD, following the large Phase 3 trials conducted by MAPS in the US, which Australia's own researchers are now building on with local populations.

**Ketamine and esketamine** appear in several trials, particularly for treatment-resistant depression. Esketamine already has a PBS listing in Australia for treatment-resistant depression as of May 2025.

**Ayahuasca** and its active component DMT are being studied in at least two Australian trials, though the regulatory pathway for ayahuasca is more complex than for synthesised psilocybin or MDMA.

## What Conditions Are Being Studied?

The breadth of conditions in Australian trials in 2026 extends well beyond the two conditions approved under the Authorised Prescriber scheme.

**Currently active trial conditions include:**

- **Treatment-resistant depression** (psilocybin and ketamine, multiple trials)
- **PTSD** (MDMA, several trials, including veteran-focused)
- **End-of-life anxiety and existential distress** (psilocybin, with palliative care partnerships)
- **Alcohol use disorder** (psilocybin, two active trials)
- **Anorexia nervosa** (psilocybin, being studied at multiple centres)
- **OCD** (psilocybin, phase 2)
- **General anxiety disorder** (psilocybin and MDMA)
- **Chronic pain** (ketamine, multiple trials)
- **Major depressive disorder with comorbid ADHD** (psilocybin, recruiting)

## What Are the Most Significant Active Trials?

**PSILODEP-2 (Monash University)**
A phase 2 randomised controlled trial of psilocybin-assisted therapy for treatment-resistant depression. This is one of the largest psilocybin trials currently running in the Southern Hemisphere.

**MAPS-Australia MDMA-PTSD**
Building on the Phase 3 data from MAPS in the US, this Australian adaptation is running at multiple sites and specifically includes veteran populations with DVA support.

**Psilocybin for Anorexia Nervosa (University of Melbourne)**
One of the few trials globally studying psilocybin specifically for eating disorders. Early results are being closely watched internationally.

**ANU Real-World Outcomes Registry**
Not a trial in the traditional sense, but a nationally significant data collection effort tracking every patient treated under the Authorised Prescriber scheme. This data will shape future PBS listing decisions.

## FAQ

**Are psychedelic clinical trials safe?**
Yes. Trials are conducted under strict ethical oversight, with detailed safety monitoring and trained clinical teams. **No serious adverse events have been recorded** among patients treated under Australia's Authorised Prescriber scheme as of September 2025, according to a TGA Freedom of Information disclosure.

**Can I join a trial if I am already on antidepressants?**
It depends on the trial. Many trials exclude participants on SSRIs because these medications interact with psilocybin. Some trials specifically recruit people who are tapering off SSRIs with medical supervision. Check each trial's eligibility criteria.

**Do Australian trials accept international participants?**
Generally no. Most require Australian residency for practical and regulatory reasons.

**Will I definitely receive the active drug?**
Not necessarily. Many trials are placebo-controlled, meaning some participants receive an inactive dose or a low active dose. Many trials offer crossover, where placebo participants receive the active treatment in a second phase.

**How long does trial participation take from application to treatment?**
Typically two to six months from expression of interest to first dosing session, depending on screening waitlists and the specific trial.
