# The Plain-English Guide to Psychedelic Therapy in Australia (2026)

Most people have heard the headline: Australia became the first country in the world to legalise psychedelic-assisted therapy. What most people have not heard is what to actually do with that information.

If you have treatment-resistant depression or PTSD, and you are reading this wondering whether this applies to you, the answer is: possibly yes. But the path from curiosity to a clinical session is not obvious. The information is scattered across government websites, buried in academic papers, or wrapped in jargon that assumes you already know what a "Schedule 8 Authorised Prescriber" is.

This guide exists to fix that. No jargon. No hype. Just what you need to know.

---

## What Is Psychedelic Therapy, Exactly?

Psychedelic therapy is not the same as taking a substance and hoping for the best. It is a structured therapeutic process that combines psychedelic substances with professional psychological support, and its effectiveness relies on three core components: preparation, the guided session itself, and post-session integration.

Think of the medicine as a key, and the therapy as the door it opens. The two do not work without each other.

Researchers and clinicians describe three distinct phases: preparation (talk therapy sessions to build trust and set intention), the psychedelic session itself (taken in a clinical setting with a trained therapist present), and integration (making sense of the experience and weaving it into daily life).

---

## Is It Legal in Australia?

Yes. Since 1 July 2023, authorised psychiatrists can legally prescribe psychedelic medicines to people with specific mental health conditions, under strict controls.

Australia did not just trial this. It changed the law. The TGA rescheduled psilocybin and MDMA from Schedule 9 (prohibited substances) to Schedule 8 (controlled drugs) for specific medical uses. For all other uses, they remain prohibited.

This makes Australia the first country in the world to establish a national, regulated clinical model for these therapies.

---

## Who Qualifies?

Currently, two conditions are eligible:

**Psilocybin** (the active compound in magic mushrooms) is approved for **treatment-resistant depression**, meaning depression that has not responded to at least two conventional treatments.

**MDMA** is approved for **post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)**.

Some people with co-occurring mental health conditions may also be eligible. However, those with a personal or family history of bipolar disorder, psychosis, or certain other psychiatric conditions are not yet considered suitable, based on current research and safety protocols.

If you do not meet these criteria today, that does not mean this space will not expand. Research into psilocybin for anxiety, addiction, and OCD is active globally, and the regulatory framework in Australia is designed to evolve with the evidence.

## How Do You Actually Access It? 5 Steps

### Step 1: Talk to your GP

Start with your general practitioner. Explain your history, your diagnosis, and what treatments you have already tried. Ask for a referral to a psychiatrist who is familiar with psychedelic-assisted therapy. Your GP cannot prescribe these medicines, but they are the gateway to the specialist who can.

### Step 2: Find an authorised psychiatrist

Not all psychiatrists can prescribe psilocybin or MDMA. Only psychiatrists registered with AHPRA with a specialist registration in psychiatry and a completed Fellowship with the RANZCP are permitted to apply for prescribing rights. Psychiatrists with an Authorised Prescriber permit can nominate themselves in the "Find a Psychiatrist" directory run by the RANZCP, which is the clearest public resource available for finding someone qualified to help.

Mind Medicine Australia also maintains a prescriber resource page at [mindmedicineaustralia.org.au](https://mindmedicineaustralia.org.au/authorised-prescribers/).

### Step 3: Assessment and preparation

Once you are connected with an authorised psychiatrist, you will go through a thorough clinical assessment. This is not a formality. The assessment determines your suitability for this type of therapy before any medicine is involved. If you are approved, a series of preparation sessions follows. These are talk-therapy sessions designed to build the therapeutic relationship, explore your history, and set a clear intention for the experience ahead.

### Step 4: The dosing session

Each dosing session typically lasts six or more hours. You will be in a clinical setting, lying down, often with an eye mask and music. One or two trained therapists sit with you throughout. You are not left alone. The role of the therapist during this time is not to direct the experience, but to hold a safe container for whatever arises.

Depending on the protocol, you may have one to three dosing sessions over the course of treatment.

### Step 5: Integration

This is where the real work happens. Without structured support after the session, the therapeutic benefits of psychedelics may be limited or even counterproductive. Integration therapy helps you understand what surfaced during the experience, translate the insights into lasting change, and process anything that felt difficult or confusing. This phase can last weeks or months.

## What Does It Cost?

This is the hardest part of the conversation.

Average prices sit at around $30,000 AUD for a full three-dose MDMA-assisted therapy program, or $10,000 AUD for a single dose with preparation and integration sessions included. The cost reflects the intensive nature of the treatment: a psychiatrist and at least one therapist must be present for every dosing session, which lasts most of a day.

That said, the landscape changed significantly in 2025.

Medibank, Australia's largest private health insurer, announced it would offer coverage for psychedelic-assisted therapy. The insurer committed $10 million AUD to fund patient access to MDMA therapy for PTSD, paired with a long-term outcomes study run by researchers at the Australian National University.

The Australian Department of Veterans' Affairs has also committed to funding psychedelic-assisted therapy for eligible veterans with PTSD, as part of a $740 million veterans rehabilitation plan.

For everyone outside those two groups, out-of-pocket costs remain significant. If you have private health insurance, it is worth contacting your insurer directly to ask whether psychedelic-assisted therapy is covered under your current policy, or whether an upgrade might make you eligible.

## What About Microdosing?

Microdosing, which involves taking sub-perceptual doses of psilocybin or LSD, sits in a different legal category entirely. It is not covered by the 2023 TGA decision. Outside the clinical prescribing pathway, psilocybin remains a Schedule 9 prohibited substance in Australia. That means microdosing is not legal under current law, regardless of the therapeutic intention behind it.

## Is It Safe?

Psychedelic therapy within a clinical setting, with proper preparation and integration, carries a meaningfully different risk profile from recreational use. In a global survey, 86% of people who had supervised psychedelic sessions reported the experience was helpful, while just 1% said things got worse. By contrast, 4.2% of those who used psychedelics for unsupervised self-treatment reported visits to the emergency department.

The clinical container matters enormously. This is not a medicine you take at home. It is a medicine you take inside a carefully constructed therapeutic relationship.

Be cautious of any provider that markets this treatment in ways that feel more like a retreat booking than a clinical referral. The regulation exists for good reason, and the TGA has already issued warning letters to clinics and media outlets for the unlawful promotion of MDMA and psilocybin.
